What does the word “Sh’himo” mean?

“Sh’himo” is the Syriac word for “common” or “ordinary.” It simply refers to the prayers that are done on ordinary days (outside of the three day fast of Nineveh and the Great Fast) when there is not an important feast of the Church being celebrated. The Sh’himo prayers do not include Sunday, as every Sunday is a festal day on which we celebrate the Resurrection (Qyomtho) of our Lord.

What are the seven times of prayer?

The Church has developed seven liturgical hours of prayer. It was King David, the Psalmist, who wrote, “Seven times a day I praise You, because of Your righteous judgments” (Psalm 119:164). The hours of prayer are as follows:

Vespers (Evening) Ramsho 6:00 PM
Compline (Night) Soutoro 9:00 PM
Nocturnes (Midnight) Lilio 12:00 AM
Matins (Morning) Sapro 6:00 AM

When does the liturgical day begin?

In the Creation account we read: “that God called the light Day, and the darkness He called Night. So the evening and the morning were the first day” (Gen. 1:5). In the Syriac tradition, the day begins with the evening (generally after 6PM). For example, Monday Vespers would be observed on Sunday evening, Tuesday Vespers on Monday evening, etc.

What are the themes that correlate to each hour of prayer?
Liturgical Hour Theme
Vespers (Evening) Ceaseless praise and uninterrupted thanksgiving
Compline (Night) Repentance
Nocturnes (Midnight) Vigilance
*The midnight hour is divided into four watches.
Each watch has various themes.		 First Watch - 'Mother of God' except on Friday when 'the Cross' is commemorated
Second Watch - 'Saints' except on Thursday when 'the Holy Apostles' are commemorated
Third Watch - 'Repentance' on Monday, Tuesday, and Thursday; 'the departed' are commemorated on Wednesday and Friday
Fourth Watch - 'General themes'
Matins (Morning) Beginning of creation' and 'resurrection of Christ'; the significance of light dawning upon creation
Third Hour Follows the theme of the day; i.e. Friday – ‘the Holy Cross’
Sixth Hour 'General themes'
Ninth Hour 'Departed'

The third, sixth and ninth hours are associated with our Fall and Salvation. They are decisive moments in the history of salvation. This is particular because at the third hour our Lord received judgment from Pilate; at the sixth hour Christ was crucified and the ninth hour our Lord gave His spirit up into the hands of His Father.

What are the themes that correlate to each day?

The Sh’himo Prayers have general themes that correspond to each day of the week:

Day Theme
Sunday Resurrection of Christ
Monday Repentance
Tuesday Repentance
Wednesday The Theotokos*
(*Greek word which means “God birth-giver”)
Thursday Holy Apostles and Saints
Friday Holy Cross, Martyrs, Confessors
Saturday Departed Clergy and Laity

On Friday, we remember the Cross as it was on Friday when our Lord was crucified. We also remember the Martyrs and Confessors who suffered for the love of Christ.

Saturday, we remember all the faithful departed. It was on Holy Saturday when our Lord entered into Hades and preached the Gospel to the departed ones.

Every Sunday, we celebrate the feast of feasts, the Resurrection of our Lord.

How should we pray the Sh’himo?
  1. Pray while standing - One of the main prayers we say throughout the Sh’himo is the “Qauma.” It is familiar to many of us as it begins with “Holy art Thou O God, Holy art Thou Immortal…” The word “Qauma” means “standing.” We stand in prayer as we are acknowledging the presence of God. We stand as a sign of reverence and respect.
  2. Kneeling during prayer - We kneel to show our submission to our Lord. For example, when we say “Crucified for us, have mercy on us”, we should kneel and make the sign of the Cross. In this book, when we see “†”, we are reminded to kneel and make the sign of the Cross. We do not kneel for the liturgical prayers on Sunday in commemoration of the joyous Resurrection of our Lord.
  3. Facing east during prayer - When we pray we should face east as it is a sign of awaiting and anticipating our Lord’s glorious second coming. Christ says, “For as the lightning comes from the east and flashes to the west, so also will the coming of the Son of Man be” (Matt. 24:27).

Standing, kneeling, making the sign of the cross, and facing east are all ways that we worship and pray with our bodies. We believe that the Holy Spirit has sanctified our bodies as the Temple of God (1 Cor. 6:19). Christ, in His Resurrection, gave us hope in the resurrection of both the body and the soul. Therefore, our bodies, along with our hearts and minds, are called to participate in prayer.

Our life as a Christian should be in communion with God, Who is love. In our communion with God, we are also brought into communion with those who loved God during their lifetime: the Saints and the faithful departed as well as our faithful brethren on this earth. Prayer for the Orthodox is personal, yet communal.

The canonical prayer has a place in God’s plan for our salvation. The liturgical hours gives us a foretaste of the life in the kingdom, where we will glorify God unceasingly. The Church has developed the Sh’himo prayers as a tool to help us organize our life around prayer. While this is only an abridged version of the Sh’himo evening prayers, may this help to strengthen the prayer life in our homes and our communion with God.

Amen.
